The Problem

Finance built on spreadsheets — at scale, it breaks

The Roku Channel (TRC) saw explosive content growth driven by acquisitions of Quibi and THOR, along with a rapidly expanding licensed content catalog. But the financial operations supporting this growth hadn't scaled. Finance teams were managing hundreds of content deals using a fragmented patchwork of Excel trackers, email threads, and manual NetSuite entries.

The consequences compounded quarterly: over/under payments to content partners, reconciliation marathons at close, and no reliable view of what Roku had spent or owed across thousands of content windows.

📊
Siloed Trackers
Each team maintained their own spreadsheet. Deal terms in one file, payment schedules in another, amortization in a third — no reconciliation between them.
💸
Over / Under Payments
Manual payout calculations across revenue streams led to systematic errors — wrong amounts sent to content partners with no audit trail to catch them.
⏳
Extended Close Periods
Quarterly financial close required weeks of reconciliation work — pulling data from multiple systems, fixing discrepancies, and re-running calculations by hand.
🔒
No SOX Controls
Without a governed approval workflow, any user could modify a payment schedule or financial window with no review gate — a compliance risk at significant dollar amounts.
The People

Who uses CFS and what they need

CFS serves five distinct user groups with different mental models of content finance. Accounting thinks in journals and periods. FP&A thinks in projections and scenarios. Content Acquisition thinks in deals and titles. Each group needed the system to speak their language — even though they were all looking at the same underlying data.

Product Vision

One system for every dollar of content spend

"Establish a single source of truth for all spend related to licensed and owned content — and enable workflows that allow tracking, initiating payments, and booking accounting entries accurately and timely."

CFS was conceived as Roku's finance backbone for The Roku Channel content spend. Rather than building a reporting dashboard, the vision was to build an operational system of record — one that every downstream finance action would flow through, from the moment a deal was signed through the final accounting journal entry years later.

Four-Year Journey

How CFS evolved, phase by phase

CFS grew from a focused MVP for Direct License workflows into an enterprise finance platform supporting Direct License, Roku Originals, and RevShare across hundreds of content partners. Each phase expanded both the workflow coverage and the data quality of Roku's content finance operations.

Phase 1 · FY22 Q1–Q2

Foundations: Deal & Asset Setup

The master brief defined CFS as a new platform to eliminate silos and repetitive data entry. Phase 1 focused entirely on the foundational financial entity: giving accounting teams a governed place to define content assets from their deal contracts.

Import deals from upstream systems (Salesforce / DLW) — no duplicate data entry
Define financial windows at the title level with contract dates, territories, and payment terms
SOX-compliant approval workflow — asset requires manager sign-off before payment schedule generates
NetSuite integration: post asset and commitment entries on approval

Phase 2 · FY22 Q2–Q4

Automating Expense Projections

With the asset foundation in place, attention shifted to automating the most time-consuming manual work: monthly expense forecasts. The DLW Excel tracker — used by accounting to project content costs — was deprecated in favor of a rules-based engine inside CFS.

Create new deals and assets directly in CFS (not just import) — covering under-consideration deals
Configure amortization rules per window: accelerated (ASC 920) or straight-line (ASC 926)
Auto-generate expense schedules; edit at window level with status tracking
Bulk CSV upload for existing forecast data during migration off Excel
Payment schedule automation: define standard or custom schedules driven by contract terms
Phase 3 · FY23

Full Payment & Accounting Lifecycle

FY23 completed the DL finance loop. Payment automation gained NetSuite integration so approved schedules flowed directly into vendor disbursements. Accounting Entries Automation eliminated the last manual step — journal creation — replacing it with a governed subledger engine.

Invoice generation from payment schedules + NetSuite integration for automated disbursements
Bulk update and approval workflow for payment schedule changes
Subledger transaction generation: asset, liability, prepaid, accrual, and expense entries
Journal entry creation and review — period controls prevent out-of-cycle posting
Roku Originals: deal tracking, payment automation, and expense forecast parity with DL

Phase 4 · FY24–FY26

RevShare, Scale & Expansion

Having proven the DL workflow, CFS expanded into RevShare — Roku's most complex payout model, where partner payments depend on monthly ad revenue, eCPM rates, and distribution model configurations across hundreds of partners. This phase required deep integration with the Ads / RAMS data pipeline.

RevShare accrual calculation engine: eCPM joins, Provider View, monthly/quarterly toggles
Deal approval workflow for AVOD agreements with batch approve capabilities
Howdy SVOD and TRC AVOD expansion — multiple distribution model support in a single view
Finance Contracts Portal: centralize contract data input across DL, RevShare, and originals
Anaplan integration for automated expense detail feeds into FP&A planning models
Design Highlights

Key UX challenges solved

Designing CFS meant navigating both the complexity of financial domain logic and the operational reality that errors here affect multi-million dollar payments. These were the defining design problems across the four-year build.

Approval Workflows with SOX Controls
Every financial asset requires a manager-level review before it can generate payment schedules or accounting entries. Designing clear role-based states (Draft, Under Review, Approved, Rejected) across multiple workflow steps without adding unnecessary friction.
Role-based access Audit trails Compliance UX
Bulk Operations on Financial Data
Accounting teams manage hundreds of assets simultaneously. Designing bulk selection, bulk update, and batch approval patterns that felt safe — not reckless — required careful affordance design and confirmation states that communicated exactly what would be affected.
Bulk selection Batch actions Error prevention
Dense Data Tables for Finance Users
Finance professionals need information density — they want to see 20+ columns, filter and sort freely, and export to Excel. Balancing the complexity of content financial data (titles, windows, currencies, territories) against readability drove all the information architecture decisions.
Data tables Filter systems Column customization
Period Close & Temporal Constraints
Financial entries are locked once a period closes. Designing the period control system — open, review, closed states with cascading effects on what editing is allowed — required close collaboration with accounting to understand the real SOX requirements vs. the operational workflow.
Period controls State machines Temporal UX
Multi-System Integration UX
CFS sits between Salesforce, RCAT, NetSuite, Anaplan, and RAMS. Designing import flows that pre-populated from upstream data while giving users the ability to correct discrepancies — without overwriting canonical data sources — was one of the trickiest interaction design problems.
Import flows Data provenance System boundaries
RevShare Calculation Transparency
Partners receive payout amounts calculated from eCPM, impression data, and revenue share percentages. Designing views that let accounting staff trace exactly how a payout number was derived — and flag outliers before disbursement — was critical for partner trust and error prevention.
Calculation explainability Audit views Payout traceability
